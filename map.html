Why did Rahm not get Chicago's votes in February?

Next: The full map of Chicago with editable parameters

Intro: a Scrolling Data Investigation

Chicago politics are fascinating. The home of Obama, Chicago boasts a strong liberal base in the otherwise-conservative Illinois.

In 2011, Rahm Emanuel won the nonpartisan mayoral election against Gery Chico and others with over 50% of the vote.

Emanuel then served a controversial first term.

In 2015, he sought office again in an election against Chuy Garcia and others. Emanuel couldn't rally a majority vote this time, forcing an unprecedented runoff against Garcia. Emanuel was elected.


What caused this change? Or more broadly: what are the key predictors of Chicago voting behaviors?

Our answer: Race.

Chicago is made up of discrete racial pockets.

These pockets often correlate to political outcomes.

We compare race to two alternative hypotheses:

    Hypothesis 1: Is political ideology the leading factor for voting behavior?

    Did people vote against Rahm because he was too conservative or too liberal?

    Is race simply correlated with general conservatism?

    There does appear to be a correlation between race and ideology.

    In order from liberal to conservative: black, then Hispanic, then white voters.

    A plot of Governor and Presidential voters colored by race ought indicate a close correlation: upper left is most Democratic, while lower right is most Republican.

    Let's see if this holds up with Mayoral data from the 2011, 2015 general, and 2015 runoff elections.

    A. 2011 Mayoral election

    We keep the Governor data on the X-axis, but swap out the Y-axis (from Presidential to 2011 Mayoral results).

    Pretty different!

    Votes are no longer tightly linked to conservatism, like before.


    ◼ Majority White
    ◼ Majority Black
    ◼ Majority Hispanic

    Based on our race-ideology correlation, we expect black and white voters to continue to hold opposing views, with Hispanic voters in the middle.

    Instead, black (liberal) and white (conservative) voters both prefer Emanuel, while Hispanic (moderate) voters want Chico.

    Thus, the race-ideology correlation no longer applies.

    A compelling explanation is that white voters like Emanuel for his conservatism and race, while black voters trust him due to Obama's endorsement. The Hispanic voters prefer Chico, the Hispanic candidate.

    The same coaltion of black (liberal) and white (conservative) voters remains for the 2015 election data.

    From 2011 to 2015, the black and white votes drop. Likely, this is because Emanual has shown through his previous term to be conservative. The Obama endorsement has been weakened.

    C. 2015 Runoff Mayoral election

    The white votes stay lower than their 2011 levels. But the black votes increase as a group to approximately the same level as in 2011.

    If black voters chose based on political ideology, they'd prefer Chuy -- the liberal candidate.

    Instead, they return to Emanuel. The interpretation is that their candidate, Wilson, was removed, so they gave allegiance to Obama's candidate.

    Hypothesis 2: Is local policy the leading factor for voting behavior?

    Did people vote against Rahm because of his prominent policies?

    Is race just correlated with public opinion on Rahm's policies?


    One of Emanuel's most discussed policies was, in 2013, closing 49 Chicago Public Schools.

    The public school closings were a controversial policy, as some claimed they disproportionately harmed underprivileged populations.

    The media suggest that school closings may have played some role in dissuading voters from supporting Rahm.1 Chuy even used the issue in his campaign.2,3

    A. Are school closings associated with overall vote?

    Not much.

    The hypothesis: Non-white voters voted against Rahm because of his policies.

    We'd thus expect to see: Non-white votes for Wilson supporting Chuy in the run-offs.

    Instead: We explained above that black voters initially vote for Wilson, then switch to Rahm, not to Garcia.

    B. Are school closings associated with change in Mayoral vote between 2011 and 2015?

    Yes.

    Those against the school vote represent the population that has realized -- and embraced that Emanuel is conservative.

    They prefer him in 2015, compared to in 2011.

    Also, the reverseis true for precincts holding closed schools.

    These conservatives disproportionately hated Obama in 2012 (showing only white-majority precincts for visual clarity).

    By 2015, these voters had figured out that Rahm was conservative. They began voting for him.

    Overall:

    Changes in votes do appear to be associated with increased perceptions of Rahm as a conservative.

    Yet these perceptions seem to affect each major racial group as a whole, rather than individual precincts.
